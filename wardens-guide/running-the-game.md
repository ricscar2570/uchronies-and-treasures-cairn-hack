> **Your role as Warden:** You are not a storyteller, a neutral arbiter, or the players' enemy. You are a **facilitator of emergent stories.** Present interesting situations (not solutions). Interpret rules quickly and fairly. React to player choices logically. Create tension through scarcity (time, resources, options). Celebrate successes, make failures meaningful.

## Core Philosophy

### Rulings, Not Rules

When a player proposes an action not covered by the rules: ask "Is it reasonable in context?" If yes, decide quickly on a mechanism (save, opposed roll). Apply difficulty modifiers if needed. Move on. Don't search the manual for 10 minutes.

*Example: "I want to use my Phase Shift Quirk to reach through the wall and unlock the door from inside." Warden: "WIL save to control the phasing. The wall is thick, so impaired if you fail." Player rolls, succeeds. "Your phased hand passes through the concrete. You reach the handle. Click. Your hand is numb for 1 round."*

### Player Skill, Not Character Skill

Reward creativity, planning, and cunning. If the players find a clever solution (avoid combat with a smart plan, negotiate instead of fighting, use the environment), do NOT require a dice roll. Success is automatic if the solution is good.

*Example: 10 guards watching a warehouse. Brute force: combat, lethal. Smart: "We trigger a false fire alarm on the other side of the complex. While they run, we enter from the back." Warden: "Brilliant. No roll needed. Guards leave. You have 10 minutes. Go."*

### Danger Is Real

Don't balance encounters to the party's strength. If players enter a Red Zone unprepared, they meet lethal enemies. If they attack 10 guards alone, they die. This isn't being cruel. It's being honest. Players learn fast: combat is the last resort.

**Don't save players from stupid choices. But warn them before suicidal ones:**

"You see 8 armed mercenaries behind sandbags. They haven't noticed you yet. You could try to sneak past, create a distraction, or retreat. What do you do?" If after this warning they charge in, let the dice fall where they may.

### Give Them Information

Information is free. Players don't roll to notice obvious things. "The corridor smells of ozone and decay. The lights flicker in a pattern: 3 seconds on, 2 seconds off. You hear footsteps, at least three people, coming from the left." Let them make informed decisions.

## Session Preparation

### What to Prepare (Minimum)

- **1 mission** with a clear objective and 2-3 possible complications
- **2-3 NPCs** with names, motivations, and one secret each
- **1 moral dilemma** with no right answer
- **The weekly accounting** (pay, expenses, debt update for each PC)
- **1 Zhou offer** (if applicable based on debt levels)

### What NOT to Prepare

- Complete dungeon maps (sketch rough layouts, improvise details)
- Predetermined outcomes (the story goes where players take it)
- Balanced encounters (the world doesn't scale to the PCs)
- NPC dialogue scripts (know their motivation, improvise the words)

### The Diamond Method

Prepare 3 things: **a starting situation, a crisis point, and 2-3 possible endings.** Between these points, the players fill the space. The session flows like a diamond: starts narrow (briefing), expands (exploration, choices), then narrows again (climax, consequences).

## Pacing

### Session Structure (3-4 hours)

| Time | Phase | Content |
|------|-------|---------|
| 0:00-0:20 | **Opening** | Recap, weekly accounting, Hayes briefing |
| 0:20-1:30 | **Mission** | Exploration, encounters, complications |
| 1:30-1:45 | **Break** | Real-world pause, Warden reviews notes |
| 1:45-2:45 | **Climax** | The central dilemma, combat if needed |
| 2:45-3:15 | **Consequences** | Debriefing, Zhou contact, downtime |
| 3:15-3:30 | **Closing** | Update trackers, tease next session |

### Signs the Pace Is Too Slow

Players check phones. Combat rounds take 5+ minutes. NPCs talk too long. Fix: skip to the next decision point. Cut the scene. "Two hours later, you reach the 27th floor."

### Signs the Pace Is Too Fast

Players seem lost. Choices feel random. No emotional weight. Fix: slow down with sensory description. Let a quiet moment breathe. Show the consequences of what just happened.

## Campaign Difficulty Curve (15 Sessions)

### Act I: Introduction (Sessions 1-6)

Yellow Zone missions. Economy pressure builds slowly. First Zhou contact around session 3-4. First Quirk around session 4-5. PCs learn the systems. Tone: tense but manageable.

### Act II: Transition (Sessions 7-9)

Red Zone missions begin. Economy becomes critical. Instability possible for PCs who played both sides. First PC death likely. Tone: desperate.

### Act III: Climax (Sessions 10-15)

Red/Black Zone missions. The central conspiracy emerges (who is deliberately destabilizing the zones?). PCs must commit: Division or Zhou. Multiple Quirks per PC. Echo transformation is a real threat. Tone: no way out.

## Creating Effective Moral Dilemmas

### The Formula

A good dilemma has: **two options, both costly, both defensible, with permanent consequences.**

### Concrete Examples

**"The Informant":** A Division informant inside Zhou's organization has been compromised. Hayes orders you to extract them. Zhou offers $5,000 to "delay" the extraction by 2 hours (giving her time to interrogate the informant first). The informant has a family.

**"The Medicine":** You find a stash of anti-rejection medicine in a Red Zone. Enough for your team for 3 months. But there's a civilian camp nearby that desperately needs it. If you keep it, you save on expenses. If you give it away, you go broke.

**"The Echo":** A temporal echo is blocking your path. But it was once Agent Reyes, a colleague you knew. It recognizes you. It says your name. It asks for help. Your orders are to neutralize all echoes.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **False dilemma:** One option is clearly better. Fix: make both options equally costly.
- **Punishing every choice:** If players always get screwed regardless, they stop caring. Fix: let good choices have good consequences (with costs).
- **Moralizing:** Don't lecture players on their choices. The system tracks Loyalty and Corruption. Let the numbers speak.

## Managing the Raines Relationship

When one or more agents begin working for Raines, the Warden takes on an additional tracking responsibility: the Division's slow, silent response.

### The Three-Mission Threshold

After an agent completes their third mission for Raines, Hayes knows. The Warden does not announce this. Hayes does not call the agent in for a conversation. The change is visible only through mission assignments.

**Starting with the fourth week after the threshold is crossed:**

- Missions assigned to the agent shift from Orange zones to Red zones by default.
- Secondary objectives become harder: extraction under time pressure, no civilian casualties in hostile environments, artifact recovery from contested locations.
- Timing windows tighten: mission windows drop from 72 hours to 48 hours without explanation.
- Hayes's tone in briefings becomes neutral where it was previously collegial. He still provides all necessary information. He stops making small talk.

### What Hayes Knows and What He Does Not Know

Hayes knows an agent is working outside the Division. He does not necessarily know it is Raines specifically. He does not act on incomplete information. He reassigns, applies pressure, and waits to see if the agent corrects course.

**If the agent reaches five missions for Raines**, Hayes files a formal surveillance request. From this point, one additional NPC agent is present on every Division mission (see Corruption 5 rules in the Loyalty and Corruption section). The two systems stack: an agent with high Corruption and five Raines missions has both the surveillance agent and the heightened mission difficulty simultaneously.

### The Raines Dilemma in Practice

Present the player with the math explicitly at session start when the threshold approaches:

> "You have completed two missions for Raines. The third will push you past the point where Hayes starts adjusting your assignments. Do you take the mission?"

This is not a spoiler. It is information the character would reasonably calculate. The player should make this choice with full knowledge of the cost.

## Managing PC Death

### At the Table

1. **Pause.** Let it land. Don't rush past it.
2. **Give narrative space.** Describe the death with gravity. Not gore, gravity.
3. **Allow a final action or words.** "With his last breath, Chen throws the fragment to Reyes."
4. **Consequences for survivors.** The team feels it. Maybe a moment of silence. Maybe rage.
5. **New character creation.** 15 minutes, during a break. The new PC arrives next scene.

### Replacement Rules

New PC starts at Recruit tier. Inherits the team's shared debt proportion. Loyalty starts at 5, Corruption at 0. Arrives with standard equipment. The Warden introduces them: a new recruit assigned to fill the gap, a scavenger who decides to join, or a prisoner freed during a mission.

## Improvisation

### Players Always Go Off-Script

Techniques for quick improvisation:

**"Yes, and...":** Accept what the player proposes and add a complication. "Yes, you can try to bribe the guard, AND he takes the money but also reports you to his supervisor."

**Steal from your favorite media.** Need an NPC fast? Take a character from a movie, change the name, drop them in.

**Ask the players.** "You enter a bar in the Yellow Zone. What does it look like? What music is playing?" Collaborative worldbuilding.

**Use random tables.** See the Tables & Generators chapter. Roll when you don't know what happens next.

**Stall with sensory details.** Need 30 seconds to think? Describe the smell of ozone, the sound of distant collapse, the way the light bends in the temporal zone.

## Safety Tools

### Session Zero (Mandatory)

Before playing, discuss: themes present in the game (violence, moral corruption, economic desperation, body horror from Quirks, PC death), individual limits (X-Card, Lines & Veils), and tone expectations.

### During Play

Keep the X-Card available (any player can tap it to skip a scene, no questions asked). Check in after intense scenes: "Everyone good? Need a break?" Fade to black for anything that makes someone uncomfortable.

### Emotional Check

After sessions with PC death, betrayal, or intense moral choices: a brief debrief. "That was heavy. How are we feeling? Anything we should handle differently next time?"
